\begin{abstract}
Learning-automata studies often require recovering the latent structure of a
Variable-Structure Automaton (VSA)---policy, transitions, and penalty
law---when only state--action--feedback interaction traces are observed. In a
P-model environment the penalty probability is assumed to depend on the action
but not on the source state; it remains unclear whether that assumption holds and
whether estimates are stable under data loss. We address this setting with a fully
empirical, count-based identification pipeline: validate the dataset, estimate
normalized probabilities from sufficient statistics, identify an optimal action and
$\varepsilon$-optimality gap, validate a forward simulator, stress-test estimates
by random row removal, and audit per-state penalties $c_k(\phi_i)$ against the
global P-model. The workflow is implemented as a reproducible end-to-end run on
$3518$ steps from $80$ sequences. On the full dataset the lowest-penalty action
is $\alpha_1$ ($c_\ast\approx 0.20$), yet behavior is only weakly
$\varepsilon$-optimal ($\varepsilon\approx 0.37$) and heterogeneous across states;
a simulator matches empirical marginals within $L_1\approx 0.016$. Under $20\%$
subsampling ($10$ trials) the optimal action and penalty ranking are unchanged and
$\varepsilon$ moves little ($0.371\pm 0.014$), while per-state penalty spreads
indicate moderate deviation from a strict action-only penalty law.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Stochastic learning agents are frequently analyzed through Variable-Structure
Automata (VSAs): finite-state controllers that select actions, update internal
state, and receive binary environmental feedback. In the \emph{P-model}, feedback
$\beta\in\{0,1\}$ indicates an unfavorable ($\beta=1$) or favorable ($\beta=0$)
response, and a widely used identification target is the action-dependent penalty
$c_k=P(\beta{=}1\mid\alpha_k)$ together with the state--action policy
$P(\alpha_k\mid\phi_i)$ and action-conditioned transitions
$P(\phi_j\mid\phi_i,\alpha_k)$. A common working assumption is that $c_k$ does
not vary with the source state $\phi_i$; in practice, however, one must infer
all of these quantities from observational interaction logs alone, assess whether
behavior is nearly optimal with respect to the inferred penalties, and judge whether
a generative simulator and a global penalty law are supported by the data.

This paper considers the inverse problem on a fixed interaction dataset with five
states, three actions, $80$ episodes, and $3518$ recorded steps. Each observation
records the sequence identifier, step index, source and destination states
($\phi_{\mathrm{src}}$, $\phi_{\mathrm{dst}}$), action $\alpha$, and feedback
$\beta$. Rather than fitting a parametric learner, we adopt a
\emph{nonparametric, count-based} scheme: empirical frequencies are accumulated,
normalized where appropriate, and checked for internal consistency. The goals are
to (i)~validate the dataset and report sufficient statistics, (ii)~estimate the
VSA parameters and derived optimality quantities, (iii)~compare simulated and
empirical marginals, (iv)~quantify sensitivity to random row removal, and
(v)~test the P-model via state-specific penalties $c_k(\phi_i)$.

The proposed workflow chains these stages in a single reproducible pipeline so that
fingerprinting, estimation, $\varepsilon$-optimality analysis, simulation,
perturbation, and model auditing share the same input and reporting conventions.
Evaluation relies on explicit metrics---$L_1$ distances on marginals, the
$\varepsilon$-optimality gap and spread of $P(\alpha^\ast\mid\phi_i)$, stability
of $\alpha^\ast$ and $\varepsilon$ under subsampling, and spread or drift of
$c_k(\phi_i)$ relative to global $c_k$.

The main contributions are:
\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
  \item An integrated empirical protocol that links validation, identification,
        generative checking, robustness testing, and critique of the P-model in
        one place.
  \item Explicit metrics at each stage so that results on the present dataset are
        comparable across runs and perturbation trials.
  \item A full empirical case study on the dataset above, including weak
        $\varepsilon$-optimality despite a clear low-penalty action, close agreement
        between simulated and empirical marginals, and stability of the optimal
        action under $20\%$ row removal.
  \item Evidence that per-state penalty estimates exhibit non-trivial spread, so
        global $c_k$ should be interpreted alongside $c_k(\phi_i)$ when assessing
        an action-only penalty law.
\end{itemize}

\section{Background}\label{sec:background}

\subsection{Variable-structure automata and the P-model}\label{sec:vsa}

A \textbf{Variable-Structure Automaton} (VSA) is a stochastic decision model in
which an agent visits discrete states $\phi_i$, chooses actions $\alpha_k$, and
receives environment feedback. In this project the state set is
$\{\phi_1,\ldots,\phi_5\}$, the action set is $\{\alpha_1,\alpha_2,\alpha_3\}$,
and each interaction step is recorded in the dataset together with a sequence
identifier and step index. The learning goal is to recover, from many such
sequences only, four objects required by the assignment:
\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
  \item a \textbf{state--action policy} $P(\alpha_k\mid\phi_i)$,
  \item \textbf{transition dynamics} $P(\phi_j\mid\phi_i,\alpha_k)$,
  \item an \textbf{environmental penalty structure} (probabilities of unfavorable
        feedback), and
  \item a \textbf{norm of behavior} (e.g.\ how often the optimal action is taken
        in each state, via $\varepsilon$-optimality).
\end{itemize}

The environment is a \textbf{P-model}: feedback $\beta\in\{0,1\}$ indicates a
favorable ($0$) or unfavorable ($1$) response. The working assumption is that
the \emph{global} penalty $c_k=P(\beta{=}1\mid\alpha_k)$ depends on the action
but not explicitly on the source state---so a single global penalty vector
$c_k$ is often used for ranking actions and simulation. Estimating
$c_k(\phi_i)$ separately in each state allows one to test whether penalties
depend on $\phi_i$ beyond the action label.

\subsection{Empirical estimators}\label{sec:estimators}

All formulas below are evaluated on a (possibly subsampled) interaction table.
Each CSV row is one step; we write $n\in R$ for row indices and $S$ for the set
of distinct \texttt{sequence\_id} values. For a fixed sequence $s\in S$,
$J_s$ is how many rows belong to $s$ (that sequence's length in steps).

\paragraph{Dataset fingerprint.}
Before modeling, the raw log is summarized by five quantities:
\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
  \item \textbf{Total number of rows} $|R|$ (one per step in the file).
  \item \textbf{Number of sequences} $|S|$ (distinct \texttt{sequence\_id}).
  \item \textbf{Mean sequence length}
        $\displaystyle \bar{L}=\frac{1}{|S|}\sum_{s\in S} J_s$
        (average of the per-sequence lengths $J_s$).
  \item \textbf{Empirical penalty rate per action} $\alpha_k$:
        fraction of rows with $\alpha(n)=\alpha_k$ that have $\beta(n)=1$,
        \[
          \frac{1}{\#\{n\in R : \alpha(n)=\alpha_k\}}
          \sum_{\substack{n\in R \\ \alpha(n)=\alpha_k}} \beta(n).
        \]
  \item \textbf{Checksum}
        $\displaystyle C=\sum_{n\in R} \texttt{step}(n)$.
\end{itemize}
On the full dataset, each per-action rate above equals the global penalty
$c_k$ used in estimation (both are the sample mean of $\beta$ given $\alpha_k$).

\paragraph{Raw counts.}
Probabilities are derived from three count tables: the empirical basis for
the policy $P(\alpha_k\mid\phi_i)$, the transitions $P(\phi_j\mid\phi_i,\alpha_k)$,
and the penalties $c_k$.
Each entry counts how many rows $n\in R$ match a pattern on
\texttt{phi\_src}, \texttt{phi\_dst}, \texttt{alpha}, and \texttt{beta}
(written $\Phi_{\mathrm{src}}(n)$, $\Phi_{\mathrm{dst}}(n)$, $\alpha(n)$,
$\beta(n)$ below).

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{$N(\phi_i,\alpha_k)$ --- actions taken in a state.}
\begin{equation}
  N(\phi_i,\alpha_k)
  = \#\{\, n\in R : \Phi_{\mathrm{src}}(n)=\phi_i,\; \alpha(n)=\alpha_k \,\}.
\end{equation}

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{$N(\phi_i,\alpha_k,\phi_j)$ --- transitions under an action.}
\begin{equation}
  N(\phi_i,\alpha_k,\phi_j)
  = \#\{\, n\in R : \Phi_{\mathrm{src}}(n)=\phi_i,\;
  \alpha(n)=\alpha_k,\; \Phi_{\mathrm{dst}}(n)=\phi_j \,\}.
\end{equation}

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{$N(\alpha_k,\beta)$ --- environment feedback per action.}
\begin{equation}
  N(\alpha_k,\beta)
  = \#\{\, n\in R : \alpha(n)=\alpha_k,\; \beta(n)=\beta \,\}.
\end{equation}
\noindent Write $r_i=\sum_k N(\phi_i,\alpha_k)$ for the row total over actions in
state $\phi_i$.

\paragraph{Conditional estimates.}
For visited states ($r_i>0$),
\begin{equation}
  P(\alpha_k\mid\phi_i) = \frac{N(\phi_i,\alpha_k)}{r_i}.
\end{equation}
For each action $\alpha_k$, transition probabilities normalize each row of
$N(\phi_i,\alpha_k,\phi_j)$ over $\phi_j$ (empty rows yield zeros). The
\textbf{global} P-model penalty is
\begin{equation}
  c_k = P(\beta{=}1\mid\alpha_k)
  = \frac{N(\alpha_k,1)}{N(\alpha_k,0)+N(\alpha_k,1)}.
\end{equation}
The action with lowest global penalty is
$\alpha^\ast\in\arg\min_k c_k$. On visited states
$\mathcal{V}=\{\phi_i : r_i>0\}$, the $\varepsilon$-optimality gap is
$\varepsilon = 1 - \min_{\phi_i\in\mathcal{V}} P(\alpha^\ast\mid\phi_i)$.
To simulate trajectories, sample
$\alpha\sim P(\cdot\mid\phi)$, $\phi'\sim P(\cdot\mid\phi,\alpha)$, and
$\beta\sim\mathrm{Bernoulli}(c_\alpha)$ along each real sequence length.

\paragraph{Per-state penalties.}
To relax the action-only penalty assumption, for each pair $(\phi_i,\alpha_k)$ with
$N(\phi_i,\alpha_k)>0$,
\begin{equation}
  c_k(\phi_i) = P(\beta{=}1\mid\alpha_k,\phi_i)
  = \frac{N(\phi_i,\alpha_k,\beta{=}1)}{N(\phi_i,\alpha_k)},
\end{equation}
where $N(\phi_i,\alpha_k,\beta{=}1)$ counts penalized steps with that state and
action. Under a strict P-model, $c_k(\phi_i)$ should vary little in $\phi_i$ for
fixed $k$; a diagnostic is $\max_i c_k(\phi_i)-\min_i c_k(\phi_i)$ per action.

\section{Methodology}\label{sec:methodology}

The implementation comprises one module per task and is run end-to-end via
\texttt{python main.py} $\rightarrow$ \texttt{load\_all} $\rightarrow$
\texttt{run\_all}. Formulas are in
Section~\ref{sec:estimators}; Appendix~\ref{app:code} lists the matching source.

\subsection{General flow}\label{sec:procedure}

The end-to-end pipeline is implemented in \texttt{main.run\_all} (Appendix~\ref{app:main}).
It always validates the raw CSV, fits the VSA quantities on the full dataset, and
optionally repeats the fit on random row subsamples to test stability. Per-state
penalty tables are printed once at the end so the console log stays readable.
Figure~\ref{fig:flow} summarizes \texttt{run\_all} with decisions and loops;
Algorithm~\ref{alg:flow} states the same logic in pseudocode.

\begin{algorithm}[htbp]
  \caption{General flow (\texttt{main.run\_all})}
  \label{alg:flow}
  \begin{algorithmic}[1]
    \Require CSV path, \texttt{perturbation\_rate}, \texttt{experiment\_count},
             \texttt{random\_seed}
    \State $df_{\mathrm{full}} \gets \texttt{load\_vsa\_dataset}(\text{path})$

  \end{algorithmic}

The implementation is \textbf{purely data-driven}: every quantity is an empirical
frequency or a ratio of counts from the provided CSV, followed by row-wise
normalization where conditional distributions are required. We do not fit
parametric models, run gradient-based learning, or impose structure beyond what
the counts support. Consistency checks (policy rows and transition rows sum to
one; global $c_k$ matches the sample mean of $\beta$ given $\alpha$) validate
the arithmetic.

The six assignment tasks form the pipeline above: a \textbf{dataset fingerprint}
(Section~\ref{sec:estimators}) validates scale and raw penalty rates;
\textbf{estimation} builds counts and conditional probabilities;
\textbf{optimal-action} and \textbf{$\varepsilon$-optimality} analyses use the
policy and global penalties; \textbf{simulation} draws synthetic trajectories
from the learned model and compares marginals to the real data;
\textbf{perturbation} randomly deletes rows and refits to test robustness;
\textbf{model critique} compares per-state penalties $c_k(\phi_i)$ across
$\phi_i$ to assess the P-model.
Subsection~\ref{sec:implementation} maps each step to code; Appendix~\ref{app:code}
lists the corresponding excerpts.

\subsection{Evaluation metrics and methods}\label{sec:eval-metrics}

This subsection records how each assignment task is evaluated before the numerical
results in Section~\ref{sec:exp-results}.
Table~\ref{tab:eval-metrics} summarizes the evaluation metrics and criteria for
assignment Tasks~1--6.

\begin{table}[H]
  \centering
  \caption{Evaluation metrics and evaluation criteria (assignment Tasks~1--6).}
  \label{tab:eval-metrics}
  \small
  \begin{tabularx}{\linewidth}{@{}c
      >{\raggedright\arraybackslash}p{2.55cm}
      >{\raggedright\arraybackslash}X@{}}
    \hline
    Task & Metric & How evaluated \\
    \hline
    1 & Raw counts &
    Report $N(\phi_i,\alpha_k)$, $N(\phi_i,\alpha_k,\phi_j)$, $N(\alpha_k,\beta)$
    by counting matching rows (Section~\ref{sec:estimators}). \\
    \hline
    1 & Policy &
    $P(\alpha_k\mid\phi_i)=N(\phi_i,\alpha_k)/r_i$ for $r_i>0$. \\
    \hline
    1 & Transition model &
    $P(\phi_j\mid\phi_i,\alpha_k)$: normalize each row of $N(\phi_i,\alpha_k,\phi_j)$
    over $\phi_j$. \\
    \hline
    1 & Global penalty &
    $c_k=P(\beta{=}1\mid\alpha_k)=N(\alpha_k,1)/\bigl(N(\alpha_k,0)+N(\alpha_k,1)\bigr)$. \\
    \hline
    1 & Consistency check (policy) &
    For each visited $\phi_i$ ($r_i>0$): verify $\sum_k P(\alpha_k\mid\phi_i)=1$
    (PASS/FAIL). \\
    \hline
    1 & Consistency check (transition) &
    For each $(\phi_i,\alpha_k)$ with transition mass: verify
    $\sum_j P(\phi_j\mid\phi_i,\alpha_k)=1$ (PASS/FAIL). \\
    \hline
    1 & Consistency check (penalty) &
    Verify computed $c_k$ equals the empirical fraction of $\beta{=}1$ among rows
    with action $\alpha_k$ (PASS/FAIL). \\
    \hline
    2 & Optimal action &
    $\alpha^\ast\in\arg\min_k c_k$; if several actions tie at the minimum, report the
    first in sorted action order. \\
    \hline
    2 & Minimal penalty &
    $c_\ast=\min_k c_k$. \\
    \hline
    3 & $\varepsilon$-optimality gap &
    $\varepsilon=1-\min_{\phi_i\in\mathcal{V}} P(\alpha^\ast\mid\phi_i)$ with
    $\mathcal{V}=\{\phi_i : r_i>0\}$ (never-visited states excluded). \\
    \hline
    3 & Interpretation &
    Answer the assignment prompts (strong optimality, noise, cross-state consistency)
    from $\varepsilon$ and the spread of $P(\alpha^\ast\mid\phi_i)$ on $\mathcal{V}$. \\
    \hline
    4 & Synthetic data &
    Generate sequences with the learned policy and transitions; sample
    $\beta\sim\mathrm{Bernoulli}(c_\alpha)$; match real row count (fixed seed). \\
    \hline
    4 & Action distribution &
    $|P_{\mathrm{data}}(\alpha)-P_{\mathrm{sim}}(\alpha)|$ per action; report
    $L_1^{\mathrm{act}}=\sum_\alpha |\cdot|$. \\
    \hline
    4 & State visitation &
    $|P_{\mathrm{data}}(\phi)-P_{\mathrm{sim}}(\phi)|$ for $\phi_{\mathrm{src}}$ and
    $\phi_{\mathrm{dst}}$ marginals; report $L_1$ sum over states for each. \\
    \hline
    4 & Penalty rates &
    $|\bar\beta_{\mathrm{data}}-\bar\beta_{\mathrm{sim}}|$ globally and per action
    ($\bar\beta$ = sample mean of $\beta$). \\
    \hline
    5 & Perturbed refit &
    Remove $\approx$20\% of rows at random; recompute $c_k$, policy, and $\varepsilon$
    (Tasks~1--3) on each subsample. \\
    \hline
    5 & Stability &
    Compare $\alpha^\ast$, $\{c_k\}$, $\varepsilon$, and simulation $L_1^{\mathrm{act}}$
    to baseline; summarize range, mean, and std across trials. \\
    \hline
    6 & Per-state penalty &
    $c_k(\phi_i)=P(\beta{=}1\mid\alpha_k,\phi_i)$ from counts with
    $N(\phi_i,\alpha_k)>0$ (Section~\ref{sec:estimators}). \\
    \hline
    6 & Cross-state comparison &
    For each $k$, spread $=\max_{\phi_i} c_k(\phi_i)-\min_{\phi_i} c_k(\phi_i)$;
    inspect full $c_k(\phi_i)$ table to assess whether penalties depend on state. \\
    \hline
  \end{tabularx}

\subsection{Discussion}\label{sec:discussion}

Taken together, the figures and tables describe a VSA that is easy to summarize
globally but not uniformly optimal in state space. A single low-penalty action
(Figure~\ref{fig:ck-baseline}) coexists with substantial slippage in
$P(\alpha^\ast\mid\phi_i)$ (Figure~\ref{fig:policy-alpha}), so reporting only
$c_\ast$ would overstate how closely the controller follows $\alpha^\ast$. The
perturbation panel (Figure~\ref{fig:eps-trials}) and summary table show that this
picture is stable under random row loss on the present sample, while the spread
chart (Figure~\ref{fig:spread-task6}) warns against reading a strict action-only
penalty law into the data. Simulation agreement in Table~\ref{tab:baseline-metrics}
supports internal coherence of the estimated model as a descriptive fit, not as
proof of a true generative process.

The analysis is limited to one log, non-smoothed counts, and i.i.d.\ row deletion that
ignores episode structure; rare $(\phi_i,\alpha_k)$ cells may still drive part of the
spread in $c_k(\phi_i)$. Global $c_k$ remain useful for ranking actions and match
per-state tables on $\alpha^\ast$, but $c_k(\phi_i)$ should be reported whenever a
P-model is discussed. Extensions on other datasets, episode-level perturbations, and
explicit state-dependent penalty models would test how far these patterns generalize.

\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}

This paper presented count-based empirical identification of a variable-structure
automaton under the P-model on a fixed interaction dataset ($3518$ steps,
$80$ episodes). A single reproducible pipeline validated the log, estimated policies
and penalties, assessed $\varepsilon$-optimality, checked a simulator, stressed the
fit under random row removal, and audited per-state penalties against the global
P-model.

The principal outcomes are that $\alpha_1$ is the lowest-penalty action with weak
$\varepsilon$-optimality overall, simulated marginals track the data closely, and
global estimates are stable under the chosen subsampling experiment, while per-state
penalties show measurable spread that qualifies a strict action-only penalty law.
Interpretation, limitations, and extensions are developed in
Section~\ref{sec:discussion}.
